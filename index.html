(un)filter

Our skin is for life, not for likes.

Filters, which began as cute puppy dog ears and love heart eyes, have now evolved into face, eye and skin color transforming toolkits for the everyday user. Bigger and brighter eyes, pointed nose, high cheekbones and filled lips, without any face fat, eye bags, wrinkles, acne or spots — Take a selfie, apply a filter, post it on Instagram. It is so simple and instant to show ourselves to the world, even though digital, as part of what is called conventional beauty standards. We all want to feel beautiful and desirable. Or sometimes we just want to hide our real appearance with filters that disfigure our face in a very funny way. We can even use piercings and tattoos by simply taking a filtered selfie. What was supposed to be ‘fun’ have now become a serious subject that needs to be talked about. The sad and worrying part of this is that filters basically diminish everything organic about our faces, our real faces, and we use it so frequently that we reach a point where we don’t like our original face anymore. The term facial dysmorphia comes under a mental health condition called Body Dysmorphic Disorder (BBD) in which a person spends hours and hours thinking about their minor or socially perceived physical flaws, whether skin imperfections, weight or a crooked smile. We live in times that likes are the new currency and achieving flawless seems to help in that regard. It’s time to say no and to get unfiltered because we are in our skin for life, not for likes.

From a website that generates AI portraits of people who don't exist (thispersondoesnotexist.com) we chose for this project a face of a beautiful girl, who could be an influencer or someone who spends a lot of time on social networks and on Instagram, and who, despite being beautiful, keeps feeling the need to be even more beautiful or to transform her appearance in several ways— that is the height of the impact that filters have on today's society.
